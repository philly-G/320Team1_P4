Who · What · Why

The Blind Spot Problem

High-stakes speaking creates a feedback gap. Presenters don't notice rising tension shaky voice, filler words, closed posture until they've already lost their rhythm.

01

The Problem

During interviews and presentations, anxiety manifests in the autonomic nervous system long before the presenter consciously feels panicked. By the time you notice, you've already lost your rhythm.

02

The Solution

SpeakEasy is a private, physiological check-engine light worn on the fingers and neck that detects rising tension and delivers a discrete haptic nudge to help you pause, breathe, and reset.

The Feedback Loop

How SpeakEasy Works

SpeakEasy moves beyond other interview tools into real-time intervention using the neck as a haptic touchpoint to trigger an embodied reset.

🫳

Monitor

EDA sensors worn on the fingers continuously track electrodermal activity sweat gland changes that signal autonomic stress before you're even aware of it.

01
🔔

Alert

When EDA spikes above threshold or speech pattern analysis detects pauses and filler word clusters a discrete vibration motor at the neck buzzes gently.

02
🌬️

Recenter

The haptic nudge at the neck prompts a natural postural correction and a breath. No thinking required the cue is physical, reflexive, and zero-cognitive-load.

03

Hardware Stack

  • EDA Sensors FingersElectrodermal activity sensors monitor sweat gland conductance, detecting stress spikes before conscious awareness.
  • Haptic Motor NeckA discrete vibration module delivers the recenter cue and naturally prompts upright posture correction.
  • Arduino BoardProcesses EDA signals, runs threshold detection logic, and triggers the haptic motor in real time.
  • Companion DashboardTracks words, pauses, filler frequency, and EDA over time synced live via USB to the analysis web app.

Conceptual Design

The Embodied Nature of SpeakEasy

The novelty of SpeakEasy lies in its rejection of traditional "Top-Down" cognitive strategies, which require the user to think their way out of a panic and in favor of Bottom-Up Embodied Regulation.

01

Addressing the Blind Spot via the Autonomic Nervous System

Anxiety manifests in the Autonomic Nervous System through sweat gland activity and heart rate changes long before a presenter consciously feels panicked. By using finger-based EDA sensors, SpeakEasy bypasses the user's cognitive delay and detects the stress response at its biological root, sometimes as soon as it has disrupted their speech.

Goessl et al., Psychological Medicine, 2017

02

Zero-Cognitive Load Intervention

Perfectionist students often struggle with Cognitive Overload during presentations. Linguistic reminders like an app saying "Breathe" can further tax working memory. SpeakEasy is a Tangible User Interface because a haptic vibration is a physical sensation that triggers a reflexive reset. This approach allows the user to regain composure without losing their place in their speech.

Lonsdale et al., 2013 · Wells et al., 2012

03

The Posture-Confidence Loop

The physical state of the body dictates the mental state of the mind. SpeakEasy places the haptic motor at the neck specifically to trigger an upright postural correction. This embodied change doesn't just look better to the audience; it physiologically signals the brain that it is in a safe, confident position, lowering cortisol levels and reducing anxiety in real time.

Peper et al., Applied Psychophysiology, 2012

Embodied Cognition

The Science Behind It

SpeakEasy's design is grounded in three evidence-based findings from peer-reviewed research.

01

HRV Biofeedback Reliably Reduces Anxiety

A systematic review confirms that tracking autonomic signals and responding in real time is an evidence-based method for lowering stress the scientific foundation of SpeakEasy's feedback loop.

Goessl et al., Psychological Medicine, 2017

02

Posture Directly Shapes Mental State

Collapsed postures increase access to negative thoughts; upright postures restore confidence. The neck-based haptic nudge is a genuine physical intervention not just an alert.

Peper et al., Applied Psychophysiology, 2012

03

One Brief Cue Can Break Arousal Mid-Performance

A randomized controlled trial with musicians showed a single biofeedback-assisted breath measurably reduces physiological stress exactly the scenario SpeakEasy is designed for.

Wells et al., PLOS ONE, 2012

Design Process

The Journey to SpeakEasy

From a pomodoro headband to a haptic wearable for high-stakes speaking --> Here's how SpeakEasy evolved:

P2

Original Storyboard

Our early vision placed our wearable in a studying context. College students often face burnout from not setting appropriate study intervals. Many struggle to break concentration out of fear of disrupting their cognitive flow. Current solutions rely on students manually setting timers or switching topics and don't adapt to an individual's fluctuating focus state. Students can also easily dismiss their timers and continue working without consequence. As a result, students lack a tangible, personalized way to shape their study sessions in support of sustained focus.

Our solution, triggered by variability in the user's EDA relative to a pre-measured baseline, would ensure personalized feedback. Because the headband responds directly from the sensor, which acted on biofeedback to regulate the user through temperature change, users would rely less on screens or conscious interactions. This would also allow our system to operate peripherally and continuously.

P2

Early Exploration

A tangible headband-based system that sets study intervals based on real-time physiological signals with the goal of supporting sustained focus through a personalized pomodoro. Early concept sketches mapped the hardware and interaction flow before any building began.

The system consists of a headband embedded with lightweight EDA sensors that continuously monitor skin conductance activity, dynamically setting pomodoro-style study intervals as an individualized "focus zone" where the user's EDA controls the session based on their arousal state.

The user is alerted to take a break through a cooling mechanism that acts as a timer, sending a physical signal that is neither audio nor physically disruptive, allowing users to finish their thought before breaking.

P2

AI-Assisted Design Concepts

We used AI generation to rapidly explore form factor directions, from active cooling bands to passive biofeedback wristbands, before narrowing to a vibration-based approach.

Our low fidelity headband prototype — a fan triggered by EDA sensor signals to physically calm the user

P2

Low Fidelity Prototype

Our first physical build was a felt headband with a fan and EDA sensors. Although we moved away from this design, it was foundational for learning Arduino and circuit work.

AI-generated concepts exploring fan-based cooling, passive biofeedback bands, modular systems, and ambient regulation

P3

Iterations & Documentation

We tested temperature-based feedback using Peltier modules wired to an Arduino, then pivoted to vibration after determining it was a clearer, more discreet signal. We also prototyped a full red fabric cape before settling on a neck-worn haptic module.
